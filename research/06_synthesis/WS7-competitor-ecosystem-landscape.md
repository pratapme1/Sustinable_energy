## Executive Summary

India's CBG market has undergone a structural shift in the last 18 months. What was a domain of pioneering mid-size private players (EverEnviro, GPS Renewables) has attracted India's largest conglomerates: Reliance Industries has committed ₹65,000 crore to 500 CBG plants in Andhra Pradesh alone, targeting 2,000 plants nationally. Adani TotalEnergies is planning 5 large-scale units (250–500 TPD each). All four major OMCs (IOCL, HPCL, BPCL, GAIL) have active CBG investment programmes.

This changes the competitive calculus significantly. The CBG market is no longer a "greenfield with no competition" — it is a market that will rapidly become crowded at the large-scale end. However, the **opportunity gap remains real and specific**:

1. Reliance is in Andhra Pradesh — not Western UP or Haryana
2. Adani's model is 250–500 TPD super-plants — a different market segment than 80–100 TPD modular plants
3. OMCs are investors/partners, not operators — they need commissioned plants, not compete to build them
4. No competitor has cracked the multi-feedstock co-digestion model with formalised supply chains
5. No competitor has formalised the digestate biofertiliser revenue stream at scale
6. The 10% LoI-to-commissioning execution rate persists — the market is won by operators who actually commission and run plants reliably

The **strategic white space**: modular 80–100 TPD co-digestion plants in the Western UP / Haryana corridor, with formal feedstock contracts, hub-and-spoke aggregation, and digestate monetisation — a model no current competitor has replicated at scale in this geography.

## 1. Competitive Landscape Map

### Tier 1 — Conglomerate-Scale Entrants (Game Changers)

#### 1.1 Reliance Industries Limited (RIL)

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Ambition | 2,000 CBG plants nationally; 500 in Andhra Pradesh |
| AP Investment | ₹65,000 crore for 500 AP plants over 3 years |
| First plant | Kanigiri, Prakasam district, AP — ₹139 crore; ground-breaking April 2025 |
| Output target (AP 500 plants) | 40 lakh tonnes CBG/year + 1.1 MT organic fertiliser |
| Feedstock | Agricultural residue, cattle dung, municipal organic waste |
| Geography (current) | Andhra Pradesh — concentrated in one state |
| Technology partner | Not publicly disclosed |
| Competitive threat level | **Extreme in AP; Low in UP/Haryana (not their focus yet)** |

**Key intelligence**: RIL has the balance sheet to execute at scale no private player can match. Their AP commitment alone (500 plants) exceeds the entire SATAT national target's commissioned count by 5×. However, their current geographic focus is AP. If they succeed in AP and expand to other states, they become a national threat within 3–5 years. A venture in Western UP has a **3–5 year window** before Reliance's national rollout potentially reaches the North.

#### 1.2 Adani TotalEnergies Bioenergy

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Ambition | 5 CBG units planned (large-scale) |
| Scale per plant | 250–500 TPD feedstock per plant |
| Investment | ₹500–600 crore per plant |
| Model | Super-scale centralised plants, not modular |
| Geography | Not publicly concentrated in one state |
| Competitive threat level | **Low for 80–100 TPD modular plants; high if they move to medium scale** |

**Key intelligence**: Adani's model is 3–5× the scale of a standard SATAT plant. Their 250–500 TPD plants require massive feedstock aggregation across a large radius. This is a fundamentally different business model — a single Adani plant aggregates what 3–5 modular plants would process. They are not competing in the same market segment as an 80–100 TPD first-plant venture.

## 4. Competitive Strategy — How to Win

### 4.1 Geography as Defence

**Western UP (Muzaffarnagar/Meerut) + Haryana corridor is the single most defensible entry point** because:
- Not Reliance's AP geography
- Not EverEnviro's Punjab straw or eastern UP press mud territory
- Not GPS's urban waste focus
- HPCL Badaun is 90 minutes away — same feedstock, same supply chain knowledge, but HPCL is not an aggregator in western districts

First-mover in this corridor with formal feedstock contracts builds a supply chain moat that latecomers must compete against.

### 4.2 Scale as Defence

80–100 TPD modular plants commission faster than 250–500 TPD plants:
- 250–500 TPD plant requires 18–24+ months to commission, land acquisition, EIA, etc.
- 80–100 TPD plant: 12–15 months
- A venture that commissions 3 plants in 3 years has locked up feedstock supply across the corridor before Reliance or Adani's super-plants are operational

### 4.3 Multi-Feedstock as Defence

Co-digestion of 3 feedstocks (manure + press mud + straw) creates supply chain complexity that single-feedstock players cannot easily replicate:
- Requires 3 separate B2B aggregation relationships (dairy farms, sugar mills, farm aggregators)
- Once operational, the supply chain is defensible — a competitor entering the same geography has to sign agreements with the same counterparties

### 4.4 Digestate as Commercial Moat

No competitor has yet built a branded biofertiliser product from digestate at commercial scale in India. Leafinti/GAIL is attempting it but not yet commercial. **First to market with FCO-certified branded digestate in the North India organic farming belt (UP, Haryana, Punjab) creates a retail channel that is difficult to disrupt**.

### 4.5 Operations Track Record as the Moat

The 10% LoI-to-commissioning ratio means the real scarcity in Indian CBG is **operating plants**, not capital or LoIs. A venture that demonstrates 3 commissioned, operating, OMC-selling plants has a track record that no competitor can substitute. This track record enables:
- Cheaper debt (IREDA rates improve with track record)
- OMC relationship priority (OMCs want supply; they prefer proven operators)
- Faster state government approvals (UPNEDA/HAREDA fast-track repeat developers)
- Equity valuation premium (proven operations vs greenfield projections)
