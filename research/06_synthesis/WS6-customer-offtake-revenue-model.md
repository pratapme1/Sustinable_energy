## Executive Summary

A waste-to-energy CBG venture in India has **four distinct revenue streams**: (1) CBG sale to OMCs at ₹54–72/kg under SATAT long-term offtake; (2) tipping fees from bulk waste generators mandated by SWM Rules 2026 to process or pay for organic waste disposal; (3) digestate/biofertiliser sale to farmers or agri-input channels; and (4) carbon credits from voluntary or compliance markets.

The multi-stream model is the core investor narrative: **no single revenue dependency**. A plant that generates all four streams is insulated against CBG price fluctuation, feedstock supply variation, and carbon market volatility. CBG to OMC is the anchor (guaranteed offtake, 15-year commercial agreement); the other three streams are margin enhancers that together can add 20–35% to top-line revenue at a mature plant.

The SWM Rules 2026 (effective April 1, 2026 — today) are the most consequential regulatory change for this venture. They legally mandated that bulk waste generators (>100 kg/day) process organic waste on-site or pay for certified off-site processing. This creates a captive customer base for tipping fees that did not exist with the same legal force under SWM 2016.

**Revenue model headline (100 TPD plant at full operation)**:
- CBG: ~₹31–38 crore/year
- Tipping fees: ~₹3–8 crore/year (if 50–100 TPD from bulk generators)
- Digestate: ~₹2–5 crore/year
- Carbon credits: ~₹0.5–3 crore/year (2025–26 prices; rising)
- **Total gross revenue: ₹36–54 crore/year** from a ~₹100–130 crore plant

## 1. Revenue Stream 1 — CBG Sale to OMCs (Primary)

### 1.1 Price Structure

| Metric | Value | Source | Confidence |
|--------|-------|---------|------------|
| SATAT floor procurement price (OMC) | ₹54/kg + applicable taxes | SATAT official; GOBARdhan portal | High |
| Contractual minimum (1 April 2024 – 31 March 2029) | ₹46/kg + applicable taxes | SATAT CBG procurement notice | High |
| Current OMC market procurement rate (2025) | ₹62–72/kg | Biofics.co.in; REGlobal industry data | Medium |
| CNG retail price (reference) | ₹80–90/kg (city-dependent) | Industry; petrol pump data | High |
| Commercial agreement duration with OMC | 15 years (extendable on mutual consent) | SATAT offtake framework | High |

**Key nuance**: The ₹54/kg is the "assured long-term offtake price" — meaning OMCs guarantee to purchase at this floor. The ₹46/kg is the legal minimum for 2024–2029. In practice, actual procurement prices in 2025 are ₹62–72/kg, significantly above the floor. The floor provides the downside guarantee; current market rates provide the upside.

**Indian Biogas Association (IBA) position**: IBA has called for a **fixed rate for CBG procurement** rather than a floating floor, arguing that price uncertainty is a barrier to bank financing. Budget 2026 submission: IBA seeking ₹10,000 crore subsidy fund and higher CBG support. [Source: biogas-india.com]

### 1.2 Revenue Calculation — CBG Stream (100 TPD Plant)

Assumptions (based on HPCL Badaun benchmark — C33):
- Feedstock input: 100 TPD (mixed: cattle manure + press mud + straw)
- CBG output: 14 TPD = 14,000 kg/day
- Operating days: 330/year (allow 35 days for maintenance)

| Price Scenario | Daily Revenue | Annual Revenue |
|---------------|--------------|----------------|
| Floor price (₹46/kg) | ₹6.44 lakh/day | ₹21.3 crore/year |
| SATAT assured price (₹54/kg) | ₹7.56 lakh/day | ₹24.9 crore/year |
| Current market (₹65/kg average) | ₹9.1 lakh/day | ₹30.0 crore/year |
| Upper market (₹72/kg) | ₹10.08 lakh/day | ₹33.3 crore/year |

**For investor modelling**: Use ₹54/kg as base case (contractually backed), ₹65/kg as realistic case (current market), ₹46/kg as downside stress test.

### 1.3 Demand Trajectory (CBO Mandate)

The Compressed Biogas Obligation creates a structured demand ramp that makes this a **policy-guaranteed market**:

| Fiscal Year | CBO Rate | Implied CBG Demand (vs current ~350,000 MT/year CNG consumption) |
|-------------|---------|------------------------------------------------------------------|
| 2024-25 | Optional | Demand = installed capacity (~2,500 MT/year) |
| 2025-26 | 1% mandatory | ~3,500 MT/year minimum demand from CBO alone |
| 2026-27 | 3% mandatory | ~10,500 MT/year |
| 2027-28 | 4% mandatory | ~14,000 MT/year |
| 2028-29+ | 5% mandatory | ~17,500 MT/year |

Note: CNG consumption is growing rapidly; absolute CBG demand will exceed these figures as the denominator grows. The CBO creates a structural floor on demand that OMCs must meet — they cannot refuse CBG from commissioned plants.

### 1.4 offtake Risk Assessment

| Risk | Severity | Mitigation |
|------|---------|------------|
| OMC refusal to purchase | Low — CBO legally mandates blending; SATAT guarantees purchase | 15-year commercial agreement; CBO enforcement |
| Price decline below ₹46/kg | Very Low — contractual minimum locked to March 2029 | Floor price is regulatory floor |
| Feedstock shortage → plant underperforms committed volume | Medium — supply chain risk | Multi-feedstock design; feedstock aggregation contracts |
| Quality rejection (low methane purity) | Low if upgrading equipment maintained | PSA/membrane systems achieve 97%+ methane — within OMC spec |

## 2. Revenue Stream 2 — Tipping Fees from Bulk Waste Generators

### 2.1 The SWM Rules 2026 Mandate — Critical Policy Driver

The **Solid Waste Management Rules, 2026** (effective April 1, 2026) are the most significant regulatory catalyst for this revenue stream. Key provisions:

| Provision | Detail | Impact |
|-----------|--------|--------|
| Bulk generator threshold | Entities generating >100 kg/day total waste, OR consuming >40,000 L water/day, OR area >20,000 sq.m. | Very large universe of eligible payers — large hotels, hospitals, malls, offices, food processors, college campuses |
| Mandate | Must process organic waste on-premises OR obtain EBWGR (Extended Bulk Waste Generator Responsibility) certificates | This creates demand for third-party CBG processors who can issue EBWGR certificates |
| Wet waste requirement | Wet/organic waste must be composted or bio-methanated specifically | CBG processing qualifies; simple composting also qualifies but CBG generates more value |
| Digital traceability | Centralised online portal for waste tracking from generation to disposal | Creates compliance paper trail — bulk generators need documented proof of processing |
| Enforcement | CPCB and state SPCBs empowered to penalise non-compliant generators | Enforcement risk makes EBWGR certificates valuable to bulk generators |

**Source**: Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change gazette Jan 28, 2026; Vision IAS / Mondaq legal analysis; CSTEP analysis.

**Investor framing**: Bulk generators contribute ~one-third of India's total solid waste. Under SWM 2026, they face either capital expenditure to install on-site processing, or recurring payment to certified off-site processors. A CBG venture that becomes a certified EBWGR processor can charge a tipping fee — transforming the feedstock from a cost into a revenue.

### 2.2 Tipping Fee Benchmarks

| Source/Location | Tipping Fee | Context |
|----------------|-------------|---------|
| Indore (municipal WtE) | ₹1,080/tonne | Cited as benchmark municipal rate |
| Delhi (WtE plants) | ₹2,000–2,700/tonne | Higher cost city; larger plants |
| India average estimate | ₹1,000–2,000/tonne organic waste | Industry composite |
| Hotel/hospital premium (estimate) | ₹1,500–3,000/tonne | SWM compliance + convenience premium; willingness to pay higher for certified processing |

**Source**: KMC (Kolkata Municipal Corporation) SWM rates 2023-24; IDR Online analysis; industry estimates. [Confidence: Medium — specific bulk-generator tipping fee data is not publicly published at granular level]

### 2.3 Tipping Fee Revenue Calculation

For a 100 TPD plant sourcing 50 TPD from bulk institutional generators (50% of feedstock):

| Scenario | Tipping Fee Rate | Daily Revenue | Annual Revenue |
|---------|-----------------|--------------|----------------|
| Conservative | ₹1,000/tonne | ₹50,000/day | ₹1.65 crore/year |
| Base case | ₹1,500/tonne | ₹75,000/day | ₹2.48 crore/year |
| Premium (certified EBWGR) | ₹2,500/tonne | ₹1.25 lakh/day | ₹4.13 crore/year |

If 100 TPD is sourced from bulk generators (unlikely at Phase 1 but possible at scale):
- Base case: ₹5.0 crore/year additional revenue
- Premium: ₹8.25 crore/year

**Strategic note**: The tipping fee model effectively makes the feedstock acquisition cost **negative** — instead of paying farmers for cattle dung or straw, the plant receives payment from institutional generators for accepting their waste. This structurally improves plant economics. In Western UP, where cattle dung is sourced from dairy farms (low or zero cost), any institutional food waste sourced at a tipping fee is pure margin improvement.

### 2.4 Bulk Generator Customer Segments

| Segment | Waste Volume | SWM 2026 Status | Willingness to Pay | Notes |
|---------|-------------|-----------------|-------------------|-------|
| 3-star+ hotels | 200–500 kg/day | Mandatory | High — brand risk of non-compliance | Clustered in Meerut, Agra, Kanpur in Western UP |
| Hospitals (50+ beds) | 100–500 kg/day organic | Mandatory | High — regulatory scrutiny | NABH-accredited hospitals have stricter compliance |
| Malls and commercial complexes | 200–1,000 kg/day | Mandatory | Medium | Food court waste particularly high-yield |
| Institutional canteens (colleges, govt offices) | 100–300 kg/day | Mandatory | Medium | Less willingness but compliance risk drives |
| Food processors / packaged food manufacturers | 1–20 TPD | Mandatory | High — already under EPR framework | Highest volume, most reliable offtake |
| APMC markets (wholesale vegetable/fruit) | 5–50 TPD | Mandatory | Medium | High moisture, consistent volume |
| Sugar mills | 50–200 TPD press mud | Mandatory (and incentivised) | High — dual revenue: tipping fee + they avoid lagoon costs | Best institutional partner in Western UP |

## 3. Revenue Stream 3 — Digestate / Biofertiliser

### 3.1 Digestate Production and Value

Every anaerobic digestion plant produces digestate as a co-product — it is unavoidable. The question is whether it is a liability (disposal cost) or an asset (revenue stream).

| Metric | Value | Source |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Digestate yield | ~80–85% of feedstock mass becomes digestate | Industry standard |
| 100 TPD plant → digestate output | ~80–85 TPD raw digestate/day | Calculated |
| Liquid vs solid split | Typically 70–80% liquid, 20–30% solid after screw press separation | Industry |
| India organic fertiliser market (2024) | USD 581 million → USD 935 million by 2031 (7% CAGR) | Global Agriculture; Persistence MR | 
| India organic farming area (2024) | 1.76 million hectares certified + 3.62 million conversion | Ministry of Agriculture | 

### 3.2 Digestate Revenue Options

| Product Form | Price Range (India) | Comparison | Feasibility |
|-------------|--------------------|-----------|-|
| Liquid digestate (slurry, direct to farm) | ₹500–2,000/ML (negligible per tonne) | Comparable to diluted liquid compost; very low unit value | Easy to start; low revenue |
| Raw solid digestate (bulk) | ₹500–1,500/tonne | Comparable to farm-yard manure | Moderate — farmers accept it |
| Processed dry digestate pellets (bagged) | ₹2,000–5,000/tonne | Comparable to organic NPK fertiliser | High revenue; requires drying/processing capital |
| Certified organic biofertiliser (FCO compliant) | ₹4,000–8,000/tonne | Comparable to certified compost/vermicompost | Highest revenue; requires certification |

**For investor materials**: Phase 1 = liquid digestate given free to adjacent farmers (reduces transport cost vs disposal; builds farmer relationships). Phase 2 = solid digestate sold at ₹1,000–2,000/tonne. Phase 3 = branded certified biofertiliser at ₹4,000–6,000/tonne once FCO certification obtained.

**FCO certification pathway**: Fertiliser Control Order (FCO) requires testing and state agriculture department approval. 6–12 months process. Once certified, product can be sold through agri-input dealers, e-commerce (BigBasket Fresho/Amazon), and directly to organic farms.

### 3.3 Digestate Revenue Calculation

For 100 TPD plant producing ~80 TPD digestate, after screw press separation:
- Solid fraction: ~20 TPD (solid digestate)
- Liquid fraction: ~60 TPD (liquid digestate, mostly water with dissolved nutrients)

| Scenario | Solid Rate | Liquid Rate | Annual Revenue |
|---------|-----------|------------|---------------|
| Phase 1 (free to farmers) | ₹0 | ₹0 | ₹0 (no cost for disposal either) |
| Phase 2 (bulk solid sale) | ₹1,000/tonne | Negligible | ₹0.66 crore/year |
| Phase 3 (bagged biofertiliser) | ₹4,000/tonne | ₹200/ML | ~₹3.0 crore/year |

Digestate is not the primary revenue driver at Phase 1 — but it eliminates a disposal cost and builds toward a significant revenue stream at scale.

## 4. Revenue Stream 4 — Carbon Credits

### 4.1 India Carbon Market Status

| Market | Current Price | 2026 Expected | Source |
|--------|-------------|--------------|--------|
| Voluntary carbon market (VCS / Gold Standard) | ₹200–300/tCO₂ (~$2–3/tCO₂) | Growing | CEEW; Costmos.in |
| India compliance carbon market (PAT + new framework) | ₹800–1,000/tCO₂ (~$10–12/tCO₂) expected | From 2026 | Costmos.in; CEEW analysis |
| Global voluntary market range | $5–50/tCO₂ | — | Industry data |
| Carbon premium for CBG (at current prices) | ~₹10–12/kg methane produced | Extrapolated from VCS rates | Medium confidence |

### 4.2 Carbon Credit Eligibility for CBG Projects

CBG projects are among the most carbon-creditable project types because:
1. **Methane interception**: Preventing organic waste from landfilling or open burning avoids CH4 emissions (CH4 = 80× more potent than CO₂ over 20 years)
2. **Fossil fuel substitution**: CBG replaces diesel/CNG in transport — direct emission reduction
3. **Additionality**: India's biogas sector is genuinely nascent; no baseline scenario includes CBG production at scale
4. **Co-benefits**: Digestate replaces synthetic fertiliser (N₂O avoided); straw processing prevents burning (PM2.5 + CO₂ avoided)

**Certification pathway**:
- VCS (Verified Carbon Standard) / Gold Standard: 12–18 months from documentation to first credit issuance
- India's Carbon Credit Trading Scheme (CCTS) framework: under development; compliance market expected from 2026
- Start documentation at plant commissioning; first credits issued ~18 months later

### 4.3 Carbon Credit Revenue Calculation

For 100 TPD plant (14 TPD CBG, replacing ~16 TPD diesel equivalent):
- Estimated avoided emissions: ~30,000–50,000 tCO₂/year (from methane interception + fossil fuel displacement)

| Price Scenario | Annual Carbon Revenue |
|---------------|----------------------|
| Current voluntary (₹250/tCO₂) | ₹75 lakh – ₹1.25 crore/year |
| Near-term compliance market (₹900/tCO₂) | ₹2.7 crore – ₹4.5 crore/year |
| Global voluntary premium ($15/tCO₂ = ₹1,250) | ₹3.75 crore – ₹6.25 crore/year |

**Note**: Carbon credits are a growing but uncertain revenue stream. Model them as upside in investor presentations, not base case. The regulatory shift to a compliance market (2026) is the key trigger.

## 6. Customer Segmentation

### 6.1 Customer 1 — Oil Marketing Companies (OMCs)

- **Who**: Indian Oil (IOCL), Hindustan Petroleum (HPCL), Bharat Petroleum (BPCL)
- **What they buy**: CBG (97%+ methane) delivered to their CGD network injection point or cylinder filling station
- **Contract structure**: 15-year SATAT commercial agreement; minimum procurement at ₹46/kg
- **Pain point they have**: CBO mandate requires them to blend CBG — they need supply; 108 commissioned plants vs 1,094 LoIs shows supply is scarce
- **How to win**: Commission plant, meet quality spec (97%+ CH4, <3% CO₂, <50 ppm H₂S), sign LoI → Commercial Agreement
- **Revenue certainty**: **Highest of all customers** — regulatory mandate + 15-year contract

### 6.2 Customer 2 — Bulk Waste Generators (Institutional)

- **Who**: Large hotels (3-star+), hospitals (50+ beds), malls, food processors, APMC markets, sugar mills
- **What they pay for**: Certified organic waste processing + EBWGR certificate for SWM 2026 compliance
- **Contract structure**: Annual waste processing agreement; tipping fee per tonne; EBWGR certificate issued
- **Pain point**: SWM 2026 penalties for non-compliance; cost of on-site processing is CAPEX-heavy; prefer outsourcing
- **How to win**: Be the nearest certified EBWGR processor; offer free pickup within 30 km radius; provide monthly compliance certificates
- **Revenue certainty**: **Medium-High** — SWM 2026 is law; but enforcement quality and bulk generator awareness varies by state

### 6.3 Customer 3 — Farmers (Digestate Buyers)

- **Who**: Adjacent farmers within 20–50 km of plant; organic farms; agri-input dealers
- **What they buy**: Liquid digestate (as liquid fertiliser) or solid digestate (as soil amendment)
- **Contract structure**: Informal at Phase 1; FCO-certified product channels at Phase 3
- **Pain point**: Rising cost of synthetic fertiliser (urea, DAP); soil health degradation from chemical farming
- **How to win**: Demonstrate digestate NPK content vs urea equivalence; free delivery radius; farmer education
- **Revenue certainty**: **Low-Medium at Phase 1; grows to Medium-High with FCO certification**

### 6.4 Customer 4 — Carbon Credit Buyers (VCS/Gold Standard)

- **Who**: International corporations meeting net-zero commitments; domestic companies under compliance (CCTS 2026)
- **What they buy**: Verified carbon credits (tCO₂e) from certified CBG projects
- **Contract structure**: Credit purchase agreements; verification by third-party auditor
- **Pain point**: Shortage of high-quality Indian carbon credits; demand from corporate ESG commitments
- **How to win**: Register project under VCS or Gold Standard at plant commissioning; sell forward to large buyers or brokers
- **Revenue certainty**: **Low-Medium now; Medium-High after India compliance market launches (2026)**

## 7. Key Revenue Model Risks and Mitigations

| Risk | Impact | Mitigation |
|------|--------|-----------|
| CBG price falls below ₹46/kg | Severe | Contractual floor; politically unlikely given CBO demand growth |
| OMC rejects CBG quality | High | Maintain upgrading equipment; PSA/membrane delivers 97%+ methane reliably |
| Bulk generators delay SWM 2026 compliance | Medium | Start with sugar mills (immediate pain point) before hotels/hospitals |
| Carbon market doesn't mature as expected | Low-Medium | Carbon credits are upside, not base case; model without them for investor stress test |
| Digestate oversupply in local market | Low | Digestate volumes at 1 plant are easily absorbed by local agriculture |
| CBO mandate reduced or delayed | Low | Would require legislative rollback; not signalled; CBO is a government priority |
