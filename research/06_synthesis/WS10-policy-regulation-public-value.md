## Executive Summary

India's regulatory environment for CBG is among the most favourable for any energy sector investment: guaranteed offtake (SATAT), mandatory blending demand (CBO), capital subsidy (MNRE CFA), concessional debt (IREDA PSL), biomass logistics support (BAM scheme), and a new regulatory mandate forcing bulk generators to become paying customers (SWM 2026). No other energy sub-sector in India has this density of simultaneous policy support.

The compliance pathway is well-defined. A CBG plant requires six categories of approvals — SPCB consent, PESO licence, SATAT LoI, MNRE CFA application, local body NOC, and grid connectivity approval. Each has a defined timeline. None is a genuine blocker for a prepared applicant; they are sequencing challenges, not policy barriers.

The public value case is strong across all four audiences — investors (policy de-risks exit), government (climate + rural employment + air quality), farmers (digestate + income from dung), citizens (cheaper clean fuel + reduced burning). A venture in this sector is simultaneously a climate asset, a rural livelihood creator, and a waste management solution.

## 1. National Policy Stack

### 1.1 Central Scheme Summary

| Scheme | Nodal Ministry | What It Provides | Relevance |
|--------|--------------|-----------------|---------|
| **SATAT** (Sustainable Alternative Towards Affordable Transportation) | MoPNG | LoI + 15-year offtake guarantee from OMCs at ₹46–54/kg floor | Primary commercial enabler |
| **GOBARdhan** | MoJS (Jal Shakti) | Integrated platform for biogas projects; waste-to-wealth focus | Registration portal; state-level facilitation |
| **MNRE Waste-to-Energy Programme** (National Bioenergy Programme) | MNRE | CFA up to ₹4 crore per 4,800 kg/day; max ₹10 crore/project | Capital subsidy — reduces equity need |
| **BAM Scheme** | MNRE | ₹564 crore outlay FY2023-24 to 2026-27 for biomass aggregation machinery; CFA ₹9 lakh per project (50% of cost, max ₹2.9 lakh) | Feedstock logistics support for straw-dominant plants |
| **DPI Scheme** | MoPNG | ₹994.5 crore for pipeline infrastructure to inject CBG into CGD networks (FY2024-26) | Reduces infrastructure cost for grid injection (Phase 2) |
| **IREDA PSL Lending** | IREDA / MoF | Priority sector loans for CBG; startup cap ₹50 crore; 15-year tenor; ~10% rate | Concessional project finance |
| **SWM Rules 2026** | MoEFCC | Mandatory on-site processing or EBWGR certificate for bulk generators | Creates tipping-fee customer base |
| **CBO Mandate** | MoPNG | 1% FY2026 → 5% FY2029 CBG blending in CNG/PNG | Guaranteed escalating demand |

### 1.2 SATAT — The Commercial Foundation

SATAT is the enabling framework for the entire venture:

**How it works**:
1. Applicant submits online application to SATAT portal
2. OMC issues Letter of Intent (LoI) within 60–90 days (for compliant application)
3. LoI enables IREDA financing, MNRE subsidy, and state facilitation
4. On commissioning, OMC executes 15-year Commercial Agreement at ₹46/kg minimum
5. CBG metered and purchased by OMC; proceeds transferred monthly

**LoI pipeline by state (2024–25)**:
- Uttar Pradesh: 55 projects in pipeline (32 operational)
- Maharashtra: 27 projects
- Gujarat: 23 projects (20 operational)
- Haryana: 17 operational

**Investor note**: The SATAT LoI is not a guarantee of commissioning — it is a conditional offtake commitment. The 10% conversion rate (1,094 LoIs → 108 commissioned) reflects developer failure, not policy failure. For a well-capitalised, professionally managed venture, the LoI-to-commissioning conversion rate should be 100%.

## 2. Regulatory Compliance Stack

### 2.1 Required Approvals — Full List

| Approval | Issuing Authority | When Needed | Timeline | Risk |
|----------|-----------------|-------------|---------|------|
| **Consent to Establish (CTE)** | State Pollution Control Board (SPCB/UPPCB/HSPCB) | Before construction | 60–120 days | Medium — EIA document quality matters |
| **Consent to Operate (CTO)** | SPCB | Before first commercial feed | 30–60 days post-commissioning | Low — if CTE obtained |
| **Environmental Clearance (EC)** | MoEFCC / SEAC | Required for plants >10 TPD capacity | 90–180 days | Medium — requires EIA report |
| **PESO Licence** | Petroleum and Explosives Safety Organisation | Before CBG compression begins | 30–60 days | Low — standard for gas handling |
| **SATAT LoI + Commercial Agreement** | OMC (IOCL/HPCL/BPCL) | Before financial close | 60–90 days for LoI; CA on commissioning | Low for prepared applicant |
| **MNRE CFA Application** | MNRE (via SATAT portal) | At LoI stage | Disbursed in tranches during construction | Low |
| **Land use conversion / change of use** | State Revenue Department | If agricultural land | 30–90 days | Medium — state-specific |
| **Grid connectivity (DISCOM)** | State electricity board / DISCOM | Apply at Month 1 | 60–120 days | Medium — can delay commissioning if late |
| **Factory Licence / Shops & Establishments** | District Industries Centre | Before commissioning | 30 days | Low |
| **Local Body NOC** | Municipality / Gram Panchayat | Before construction | 30 days | Low |
| **Water extraction NOC** | State Ground Water Board | If using groundwater | 30–60 days | Low |
| **VCS / Gold Standard Registration** | Verra / Gold Standard Foundation | At commissioning | 12–18 months to first credits | Low — parallel process |

### 2.2 Approval Sequencing (Critical Path)

```
Month 1–2: Land secured + SPCB CTE application filed + DISCOM grid application
Month 2–3: SATAT LoI obtained + MNRE CFA application submitted
Month 3–4: SPCB CTE granted + Environmental Clearance obtained
Month 4–5: IREDA loan application filed (requires LoI + CTE + land docs)
Month 5–6: Financial close + EPC contract signed + PESO licence applied
Month 6: Construction begins
Month 15–16: SPCB CTO applied (plant 80% complete)
Month 17: PESO licence for compression
Month 17–18: Commissioning + OMC Commercial Agreement executed
Month 18: First CBG sold; MNRE final tranche disbursed
```

**Common delay points** (and how to avoid):
- SPCB CTE: Submit a high-quality EIA report; engage state-empanelled environmental consultant; use UPNEDA/HAREDA facilitation
- DISCOM grid connectivity: Apply in Month 1; it has the longest tail outside of EC
- Land use conversion: Use state government land (UP offers free land) to bypass entirely

## 4. Public Value Case

### 4.1 For Government (Policy Alignment)

A CBG plant in Western UP directly advances 7 national and state government priorities simultaneously:

| Government Priority | How This Venture Contributes | Quantified Impact (per 100 TPD plant) |
|--------------------|---------------------------|--------------------------------------|
| **Swachh Bharat (Clean India)** | Processes 30,000+ tonnes/year of organic waste instead of landfilling | Diverts ~33 TPD from landfills |
| **SATAT target** (5,000 CBG plants) | Adds one commissioned plant to the 108 currently operational | Contributes ~14 TPD CBG to national supply |
| **CBO compliance** | Produces CBG that OMCs must purchase to meet mandatory blending obligation | Reduces OMC CBO compliance gap |
| **Air quality (NCAP)** | Prevents paddy straw burning (supplemental feedstock); prevents landfill methane | ~1,000 MT straw not burned per season; prevents ~30,000 tCO₂e/year methane |
| **Rural employment** | 30–40 direct jobs; 100+ indirect (feedstock supply chain, digestate distribution) | 30–40 direct jobs in rural/peri-urban UP |
| **Farmer income** | Digestate returned to farmers as biofertiliser; dung has a value price | ₹500–2,000/tonne digestate value to adjacent farms |
| **Energy security** | Domestic CBG substitutes CNG imports | ~4,600 MT CBG/year (14 TPD × 330 days) = domestic gas production |

### 4.2 For Investors (Policy De-Risking)

The density of policy support transforms this from a market bet into a **policy-backed infrastructure play**:

- Demand de-risked: CBO mandate creates guaranteed purchase obligation on OMCs
- Price de-risked: ₹46/kg contractual floor through FY2029; market rates currently ₹62–72/kg
- Capital de-risked: MNRE CFA (₹10 crore grant) + IREDA PSL debt reduces equity required
- Operations de-risked: UPNEDA single-window clearance; state land incentive
- Exit de-risked: CBG infrastructure is strategic asset; OMC JV acquisition or RE infrastructure fund acquisition are natural exit routes

**Investor sentence**: "This is a government-mandated, OMC-offtaked, CBG infrastructure venture — not a bet on green energy markets."

### 4.3 For Citizens (Local Benefit Narrative)

| Benefit | Who Benefits | Tangible Form |
|---------|------------|--------------|
| Cheaper clean cooking/transport fuel | Urban consumers near CGD network | CBG priced below CNG at retail |
| Cleaner air | NCR + Punjab/Haryana belt | Straw not burned; landfill methane captured |
| Farmer income | Dairy farmers, sugar mills, agri communities | Dung monetised; digestate = fertiliser savings |

### 4.4 Methane Emissions — The Climate Argument

| Emission Source Addressed | Volume | GHG Impact |
|--------------------------|--------|-----------|
| Landfill methane (from waste diversion) | 30,000+ tonnes organic waste/year | ~24,000–30,000 tCO₂e/year avoided |
| Paddy straw burning (from straw-as-feedstock) | ~3,000 tonnes straw/season | ~4,500 tCO₂e/year avoided |
| Fossil CNG substitution (CBG replacing diesel/CNG) | 4,600 MT CBG/year | ~10,000–12,000 tCO₂e/year |
| **Total per plant** | | **~35,000–47,000 tCO₂e/year avoided** |

India's waste sector contributes 15% of national methane emissions (C17). A 100 TPD CBG plant captures methane from organic waste that would otherwise decompose anaerobically in landfills. At 80× warming potential (20-year GWP), every tonne of methane captured = 80 tonnes CO₂e avoided.

## 5. Regulatory Risks

| Risk | Probability | Severity | Mitigation |
|------|------------|---------|-----------|
| SATAT floor price reduced below ₹46/kg | Very Low | High | Contractual floor locked to March 2029; regulatory rollback politically costly given CBO mandate |
| SPCB consent withheld or delayed | Low-Medium | Medium | Engage SPCB in Month 1; use UPNEDA facilitation; high-quality EIA |
| Land acquisition delays (private land) | Medium | Medium | Use UP state land offer (eliminates private acquisition risk) |
| CBO mandate diluted or deferred | Very Low | High | CBO is gazetted; enforcement mechanism in place; OMCs have committed to CBG procurement |
| New environmental regulation adding CAPEX | Low | Low | CBG plants are classified as green infrastructure; regulatory direction is pro-CBG |
| SWM 2026 enforcement weak (bulk generator compliance low) | Medium | Low | Tipping fee is upside, not base case; plant economics work without it |

## 7. Key Judgments

| Judgment | Basis | Confidence |
|----------|-------|------------|
| Policy environment is the strongest in any Indian energy sector right now | CBO mandate + SATAT floor + MNRE CFA + IREDA PSL + SWM 2026 + state land incentives = unprecedented density | High |
| The regulatory stack is manageable — not a barrier, a sequencing challenge | 108 plants commissioned prove the process works; delays are applicant-preparation failures | High |
| UP state land offer eliminates the highest-risk CAPEX item (land acquisition) | Free land = no private negotiation risk; no title disputes; faster construction start | High |
| The public value case is strong enough for a government co-investment or grant | 7 national priorities advanced per plant; measurable GHG, employment, air quality impact | High |
| CBO mandate is the most durable policy support — harder to roll back than subsidies | It is a statutory obligation on OMCs; rolling it back would require OMCs to under-purchase domestically and import more | High |
