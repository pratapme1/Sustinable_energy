## Purpose of This Workstream

WS1 established that India's waste problem is large, verified, and costly. WS2 answers a more specific question: **which exact waste streams are worth targeting, and why?**

This matters because "waste" is not one market. Paddy straw, kitchen scraps, cattle dung, and press mud behave completely differently in terms of collection, chemistry, technology fit, and venture economics. Treating them as one category produces a weak pitch and poor execution decisions.

---

## India CBG Potential by Feedstock — The Headline Number

**Source**: Centre for Science and Environment (CSE) / World Biogas Association India Market Report 2023

India's total CBG (Compressed Biogas) production potential from all organic waste is estimated at **62 million metric tonnes per annum (MMT/year)**.

| Feedstock Category | CBG Potential (MMT/year) | Share of Total |
|---|---|---|
| Cattle dung + livestock manure | 25 MMT | 40% |
| Agricultural residues | 20 MMT | 32% |
| Sewage treatment plants (sludge) | 10 MMT | 16% |
| Municipal solid waste (organic) | 5 MMT | 8% |
| Press mud + spent wash | 2 MMT | 3% |
| **Total** | **62 MMT** | **100%** |

**Important**: Current installed CBG capacity as of March 2023 = **9,019 tonnes total produced** across 58 commissioned plants. That is less than **0.015%** of the theoretical potential. The gap is not a sign of technical failure — it is a sign of how early this market is.

## Feedstock Taxonomy — 6 Classes

### Class 1: Municipal Wet Organic Waste

**What it is**: Household kitchen waste, vegetable market waste, hotel/restaurant food waste, institutional canteen waste (colleges, hospitals, corporate campuses, malls)

**Volume** [Fact — High Confidence]:
- Total India MSW: ~160,000 TPD
- Organic fraction: 40–55% average → **~64,000–88,000 TPD organic wet waste**
- Hyderabad alone generates 400–500 TPD food waste daily (Wikifarmer / Spring Nature)
- Current CBG potential from MSW: **5 MMT/year** (CSE 2023)

**Physical characteristics**:
- Moisture content: 70–85% (high — ideal for AD)
- Volatile solids: 80–90% of total solids
- Biogas yield: **400–800 L/kg VS** (highest of all common feedstocks)
- Methane content: 55–65%
- Contamination risk: HIGH — plastic, glass, inert mixed in at source in most Indian cities

**Collection profile**:
- Urban — door-to-door collection infrastructure exists (Swachh Bharat)
- Source segregation remains poor except in Tier-1 cities with SWM Rules 2016 enforcement
- Hotels, markets, institutions offer **concentrated, pre-segregated** waste — lowest contamination risk
- Best collection entry point: **institutional and commercial generators** (hotels, malls, food courts, hospitals, market yards), not household mixed waste

**Disposal pain level**: HIGH — municipalities pay per tonne for disposal; landfills are overloaded

**Venture attractiveness**: ★★★★☆
Strong biogas yield, concentrated supply at institutions, tipping fee revenue possible, policy backed

**Key risk**: Source segregation quality for household waste. Institutional waste is a better early entry.

### Class 2: Livestock Manure (Cattle, Buffalo, Poultry)

**What it is**: Dung and slurry from cattle, buffalo, pigs, poultry. Includes dairy farm effluent, gaushala waste.

**Volume** [Fact — High Confidence]:
- India has 512 million livestock, ~300 million bovines
- CBG potential from cattle dung alone: **25 MMT/year** (40% of India's total potential)
- Animal + poultry waste = **41%** of India's total biomass CBG potential (CSE)
- India dairy farm clusters: Anand (Gujarat), Karnal (Haryana), Ludhiana (Punjab), Pune (Maharashtra), Bangalore (Karnataka)

**Physical characteristics**:
- Moisture: 75–95% (slurry form — direct AD input, no preprocessing needed)
- Biogas yield: **200–300 L/kg VS** (cattle); **300–500 L/kg VS** (poultry — higher nitrogen)
- Production rate: ~10 kg dung/day per adult bovine
- Year-round, non-seasonal, predictable — unlike crop residue
- Digestate: high-quality biofertiliser (vermicompost grade)

**Collection profile**:
- Dairy farms and gaushalas = concentrated, already aggregated at one location
- Farmers already manage manure but do not monetise it
- Rural collection — no urban infrastructure needed
- Logistics: low complexity at dairy clusters; moderate at dispersed villages

**Disposal pain level**: MEDIUM — manure is a nuisance but not a regulatory crisis yet

**Venture attractiveness**: ★★★★★
Highest CBG potential, no preprocessing, year-round supply, concentrated at dairy farms, low collection cost, digestate as second revenue stream. **This is the most bankable near-term feedstock for an AD-based venture.**

**Key risk**: Ownership and payment model with farmers/dairy cooperatives. Revenue sharing must be attractive enough to formalise collection.

### Class 3: Agricultural Residue (Paddy Straw, Wheat Straw, Cotton Stalks, Corn Cobs)

**What it is**: Post-harvest field residue from cereal and fibre crops — paddy straw, wheat straw, cotton stalks, maize cobs, groundnut shells, soybean stalks

**Volume** [Fact — Medium Confidence]:
- Total India crop residue: **500–650 MT/year** (NITI Aayog 2023)
- Paddy straw specifically: **~100–120 MT/year** (Punjab 19-20 MT; UP ~30 MT; others)
- Surplus residue (not used on-farm): **~140–160 MT/year** estimated
- CBG potential from agricultural residues: **20 MMT/year** (CSE 2023)

**Physical characteristics**:
- Moisture: **10–25%** (dry — opposite of food waste)
- Lignocellulosic structure: high cellulose (32–47%), lignin (5–24%) → **resistant to AD without pretreatment**
- Biogas yield (untreated paddy straw): **~211 L/kg DM** (low; ScienceDirect 2022)
- Biogas yield (with pretreatment): **32–120% improvement** (steam explosion, alkali, fungal)
- Better suited to: **gasification, pyrolysis, briquetting, co-digestion with manure**
- Co-digestion with cattle manure significantly improves biogas yield from straw

**Collection profile**:
- **SEASONAL**: Paddy harvest Oct–Nov; Wheat Apr–May. 6-month gaps.
- **DISPERSED**: Across millions of small farms; average Indian farm = 1.08 ha
- Logistics: High cost to aggregate from dispersed rural sources
- Competition: Straw also used for cattle fodder, thatching, paper industry
- Haryana Policy 2023: State actively paying to redirect straw from burning to energy use

**Disposal pain level**: VERY HIGH — burning is the cheapest option for farmers right now; it costs them nothing and clears fields fast. Converting this pain requires **farmer incentive design**, not just technology.

**Venture attractiveness**: ★★★☆☆
Enormous volume, strong policy push, but logistics-heavy, seasonal, requires preprocessing. **Second-layer opportunity, not first wedge.** Best approach: co-digestion with manure (using straw to enhance existing AD plants), or dedicated gasification/pyrolysis cluster at aggregation points.

**Key risk**: Aggregation logistics and cost. Preprocessing adds CAPEX. Seasonal supply requires storage infrastructure.

### Class 4: Agro-Industrial Byproducts (Press Mud, Spent Wash, Distillery Effluent, Bagasse)

**What it is**: Organic byproducts from food and agri-processing industries — sugar mills (press mud, bagasse, molasses), distilleries (spent wash), fruit and vegetable processors, dairy processors

**Volume** [Fact — High Confidence]:
- Sugarcane bagasse: **~100 MT/year** produced in India
- Press mud: **~12 MT/year** (3 tonnes per 100 tonnes cane crushed)
- Sugar mills: India has **~730 operational sugar mills**
- Distillery spent wash: Significant volume — each litre of alcohol produces 8–15 litres spent wash
- CBG potential from press mud + spent wash: **2 MMT/year** (CSE) — likely conservative
- 44 bio-CNG plants already operating at sugar mills across 13 states (as of 2023)

**Physical characteristics**:
- Press mud: high moisture (70–75%), rich in sugars, waxes, organic matter — **excellent AD feedstock**
- Bagasse: dry, lignocellulosic — better for co-generation (already used by most mills)
- Spent wash: very high organic load (COD: 80,000–120,000 mg/L) — excellent biogas but requires UASB reactor
- Biogas yield from press mud: high (comparable to food waste)

**Collection profile**:
- Already concentrated at sugar mill / distillery premises
- No collection logistics needed — waste is generated at one fixed industrial site
- Many sugar mills already have biogas plants or CBG units on-premises
- Pre-existing relationship between mill operator and waste — straightforward partnership

**Disposal pain level**: HIGH for distilleries (spent wash is a major pollutant; PCB fines apply)

**Venture attractiveness**: ★★★★☆
Concentrated feedstock, high yield, no collection logistics, regulatory pressure pushes mills to act. **Best as a B2B technology/service model** — partner with mills, don't own feedstock. Strong entry point in Maharashtra, UP, Karnataka (major sugar states).

**Key risk**: Sugar mills already have partial solutions; getting exclusive offtake agreements competitive. Market partially served by existing players.

### Class 5: Food Processing and Institutional Organic Waste

**What it is**: Waste from food processing units (vegetable/fruit processors, packagers, abattoirs), large institutional kitchens (railways, defence, hospitals, universities), large hotel chains

**Volume** [Fact — Medium Confidence]:
- India's food waste: **78.2 MT/year total** (WRI India / UNEP 2024)
- Post-harvest and processing losses: significant fraction of total food waste
- Slaughterhouse biogas potential: **739 million m³ biomethane/year** (RSC Sustainable Energy 2023)
- Hotels and restaurants: concentrated waste streams at fixed locations

**Physical characteristics**:
- High moisture, high organic content — similar to municipal food waste
- Fruit/vegetable processing waste: biomethanation potential 0.303 L/g VS (Springer 2022)
- Slaughter waste: blood, organs, fat — high biogas yield but requires dedicated handling

**Collection profile**:
- Fixed industrial/institutional locations = low collection complexity
- Volumes predictable and consistent
- Often regulated — businesses need disposal solutions (creates willingness to pay tipping fees)

**Disposal pain level**: HIGH for food processors (waste disposal cost; regulatory pressure)

**Venture attractiveness**: ★★★★☆
Concentrated, consistent, high-yield, regulated drivers. Particularly strong for:
- **Abattoir clusters** (Deonar Mumbai, Ghazipur Delhi, Rampura Phul Haryana)
- **APMC wholesale market yards** (massive daily fruit/vegetable waste)
- **Railway catering** (Indian Railways generates enormous institutional food waste)

### Class 6: Sewage Sludge (STP Organic)

**What it is**: Sludge from sewage treatment plants and biosolids from urban wastewater treatment

**Volume** [Fact — Medium Confidence]:
- India sewage generation: **72,368 MLD** (million litres per day)
- Only **20,235 MLD has treatment capacity** — 28% of generated sewage
- CBG potential from STPs: **10 MMT/year** (CSE — third largest single feedstock category)
- Most existing STPs with digesters flare their biogas rather than utilise it

**Physical characteristics**:
- High water content, stabilised organic fraction
- Good AD feedstock but lower biogas yield than food waste
- Co-digestion with food waste significantly improves yield

**Collection profile**:
- Already at centralised STP locations — zero collection logistics
- Requires partnership with Urban Local Body (ULB) or private STP operator
- Capital-intensive entry — STP infrastructure already exists but needs biogas upgrade

**Disposal pain level**: HIGH — sludge disposal is a regulatory problem for ULBs

**Venture attractiveness**: ★★★☆☆
Large potential, concentrated, but capital-heavy and requires government partnership model. Better suited to a later-stage venture than a first-entry beachhead.

## Key Finding: The First-Wedge Recommendation

**Based on verified data, the strongest first-wedge feedstock combination is:**

### 1. Cattle/Livestock Manure — Primary Feedstock
- Highest CBG potential (25 MMT/year)
- No preprocessing, year-round, concentrated at dairy farms
- India's dairy cooperative infrastructure (AMUL model) provides a ready partnership template
- Digestate = biofertiliser for farmers = second revenue stream
- Already proven at small scale (NNBOMP biogas programme)

### 2. Institutional Wet Organic Waste — Co-feedstock / Urban Entry
- Hotels, market yards, APMC mandis, hospitals, universities
- Pre-segregated at source = better quality than household MSW
- Tipping fee revenue + energy revenue
- Urban proximity = easier offtake for CBG/gas grid

### 3. Press Mud + Spent Wash — Industrial B2B Entry
- Zero collection logistics, concentrated volume
- Sugar mill partnerships in Maharashtra, UP, Karnataka
- 44 plants already operating = proven model to replicate/scale

### Agri Residue (Paddy Straw) — Second Horizon
- Enormous potential but logistics-heavy and seasonal
- **Best approach**: co-digest with manure in existing AD plants; OR dedicated gasification unit at aggregation node
- Do not start here — too operationally complex for a first-wedge venture
