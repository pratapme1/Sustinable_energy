## Executive Summary

India's waste-to-energy market is a USD 1.64 billion market in 2024, growing at 10.2% CAGR to USD 3.49 billion by 2032. The CBG-specific opportunity is structurally larger: India has a sustainable CBG production potential of 90 billion cubic metres per year — equivalent to 120% of the country's entire natural gas demand. Only 108 commercial CBG plants have been commissioned nationally as of July 2025, against 1,094 letters of intent. Market capture is under 0.015% of potential.

The market is not uniformly distributed. Five geographic clusters — Western Uttar Pradesh, Punjab's straw belt, Gujarat's dairy cooperative corridor, Haryana's agri-dairy corridor, and Maharashtra's sugar belt — collectively account for the majority of India's accessible, concentrated, high-yield feedstock. These are the locations where a waste-to-energy venture can achieve defensible supply chains, lowest logistics cost, and fastest path to first revenue.

**Top beachhead recommendation**: Western Uttar Pradesh (Muzaffarnagar–Meerut–Saharanpur triangle) — multi-feedstock, most CBG plants already operational, government land incentives, proven HPCL benchmark adjacent.

### 1.2 Demand Catalyst — Compressed Biogas Blending Obligation (CBO)

The CBO is the single most important demand-side development in India's CBG market. It mandates CBG blending into CNG/PNG networks:

| Fiscal Year | CBO Mandate | Network Context |
|-------------|-------------|-----------------|
| 2024-25 | Optional | Voluntary phase |
| 2025-26 | 1% mandatory | ~11 million CNG vehicles in India |
| 2026-27 | 3% mandatory | Rapidly expanding CGD network |
| 2027-28 | 4% mandatory | 630+ districts under CGD |
| 2028-29 | 5% mandatory | Full network obligation |

**Investor implication**: The CBO creates guaranteed offtake demand that ramps 5× between 2025 and 2029. This is a captive, growing, price-regulated market. CBG producers with plants commissioned in 2025-26 are positioned at the front of the demand curve.

Source: SATAT / MoPNG Gazette Notification; WBA India Policy and Market Briefing 2024.

## 3. Cluster-Level Analysis

### Cluster 1: Western Uttar Pradesh — Multi-Feedstock Triangle

**Districts**: Muzaffarnagar, Meerut, Saharanpur, Bijnor, Bulandshahar, Aligarh, Badaun (adjacent)
**State score**: 23/25 — #1 nationally

**Why this cluster leads:**
- UP state alone has potential for 1,000 CBG plants (20% of the SATAT 5,000 target) from just 20% of its surplus feedstock — CSE analysis
- Western UP districts have the highest feedstock density in the state: sugar mills (press mud + spent wash + bagasse), dairy farms (UP generates ~3 MMT/day cow dung statewide), paddy/wheat straw
- This is India's largest sugar-producing region — agro-industrial feedstock is concentrated and available year-round
- 32 CBG plants commissioned (most in any state); 113 more under construction

**Real-world benchmark in this cluster**:
- HPCL Badaun CBG Plant (Badaun, UP): commissioned January 2024
- Scale: 100 TPD paddy straw input → 14 TPD CBG output + 65 TPD solid manure
- Investment: ₹133 crore on 50 acres
- This is the most credible, recent, Indian-built reference point for straw-to-CBG economics

**Government incentives specific to UP (2024)**:
- CM Yogi Adityanath announced state will offer land (free/subsidised) for cow dung-based CBG plants
- UPNEDA (UP New and Renewable Energy Development Agency) has active CBG development program
- UP state bioenergy policy includes preferential grid connectivity + CAPEX support

**Feedstock profile**:
| Feedstock | Estimated Volume | Seasonality |
|-----------|-----------------|-------------|
| Paddy/wheat straw | Western UP: ~4-6 MT/year | Oct-Nov and Apr-May harvest peaks |
| Sugar press mud | Major sugar mills: Muzaffarnagar, Meerut, Saharanpur | Nov-April crushing season |
| Cattle manure | Continuous (dairy clusters + smallholder farms) | Year-round |
| Institutional food waste | Meerut, Aligarh city centers | Year-round |

**Risk**: Straw feedstock is seasonal — requires storage infrastructure. Mitigation: anchor feedstock = cattle manure (year-round), straw as supplemental blend.

### Cluster 2: Punjab Straw Belt — Residue Interception Opportunity

**Districts**: Sangrur (12.73%), Ferozpur (10.62%), Amritsar (10.30%), Patiala (8.54%), Ludhiana (8.21%)
*Note: percentages = share of Punjab's total district-level straw potential*
**State score**: 17/25

**Why this cluster matters:**
- 19–20 MT paddy straw generated annually; 85–90% currently burned in fields (C9)
- Burning causes 44,000–98,000 premature deaths/year nationally; Punjab/Haryana contribute 48% of burning emissions (C10, C12)
- Only 97.5 MW of biomass power currently operational (consuming 8.8 lakh MT straw/year) against 2 GW potential from 20 MT feedstock — utilisation rate <5%
- Haryana Paddy Straw Policy 2023 targets zero burning by 2027; Punjab follows similar political pressure

**Sangrur District** — top priority sub-district:
- Highest straw potential of all Punjab districts (12.73% of state total)
- ~2.4 MT paddy straw/year in Sangrur alone
- At HPCL Badaun yields (14 TPD CBG per 100 TPD straw), Sangrur could support 4–5 CBG plants of 100 TPD scale

**Risks specific to Punjab**:
- Straw is seasonal (Oct-Nov peak); must blend with year-round feedstock or build 120–150 day storage
- Procurement competition: biomass power plants already purchasing straw; price pressure exists
- HPCL and IOC already active in state — first-mover advantage narrowing

**Strategic positioning**: Punjab is a Phase 2 cluster, not Phase 1 beachhead. First-entry via cattle manure (year-round) in Western UP; expand to Punjab straw as volume partner after operations stabilise.

---

### Cluster 3: Gujarat Dairy Cooperative Corridor

**Districts**: Anand, Banaskantha, Mehsana, Sabarkantha (AMUL basin); Surat (sugar belt)
**State score**: 19/25

**The AMUL/Banas Dairy Benchmark:**
- Banas Dairy (Banaskantha, Gujarat): operational 40 TPD CBG plant
- Output: 300 MT CBG/year from dairy waste
- This is India's most prominent cooperative-model CBG deployment
- Proves the B2B cooperative model: dairy union aggregates cattle dung → CBG → sells to OMC

**Why Gujarat is a high-priority cluster:**
- 20 CBG plants operational — second-highest state
- Cooperative infrastructure (AMUL network, Dudhsagar Dairy, etc.) solves the feedstock aggregation problem
- Gujarat's CGD network is among the most mature in India — CBG can be injected readily
- Sugar belt (Surat, Bharuch, Narmada) provides press mud + spent wash as supplemental feedstock
- State has clear bioenergy policy + GEDA (Gujarat Energy Development Agency) active

**Feedstock profile**:
| Feedstock | Volume | Aggregator |
|-----------|--------|------------|
| Cattle dung | ~8-10 MT/year in dairy belt | Cooperative unions (AMUL, Banas) |
| Sugar press mud | Seasonal (Surat/Bharuch belt) | Sugar mills |
| Institutional food waste | Ahmedabad, Surat, Vadodara | Municipalities (SWM Rules 2016 mandate) |

**Competitive risk**: Gujarat is the most "discovered" CBG cluster in India. AMUL already has its own model. Entry strategy here should be B2B partnership with smaller cooperative unions, not direct competition with Banas Dairy.

---

### Cluster 4: Haryana Agri-Dairy Corridor

**Districts**: Karnal, Kurukshetra, Kaithal, Fatehabad, Jind, Hisar, Sirsa
**State score**: 19/25

**Why Haryana is strategically important:**
- 11,559 MJ bioenergy from crop residue — second highest in India after Punjab
- 17 CBG plants operational — 3rd highest state
- Paddy Straw Policy 2023: zero burning target by 2027 = regulatory mandate driving feedstock-to-CBG conversion
- National Highway 44 (Delhi-Chandigarh) runs through this belt — transport logistics for CBG cylinders/pipeline injection

**Karnal District** — top sub-district:
- Major dairy hub (National Dairy Research Institute, Karnal)
- Paddy + wheat straw + cattle manure = tri-feedstock cluster
- Less competed than Western UP; land cost lower than Punjab near Chandigarh

**Synergy with Punjab**: Haryana's southern straw districts (Kaithal, Kurukshetra) are adjacent to Punjab's Patiala/Ludhiana. A venture with plants in both states can share logistics infrastructure — one aggregate supply chain serving multiple plants.

---

### Cluster 5: Maharashtra Sugar Belt + MSW

**Districts**: Nashik, Kolhapur, Solapur, Sangli, Pune (Pimpri-Chinchwad industrial zone)
**State score**: 13/25

**Why Maharashtra is a later-stage cluster:**
- Largest MSW generator nationally (22,632 TPD) but MSW-to-energy has high contamination risk — a Phase 3 play, not beachhead
- Sugar belt is different: 44 bio-CNG plants proven nationally at sugar mills; Maharashtra has significant mill concentration
- Bagasse (100 MT/year nationally), press mud (12 MT/year nationally), spent wash — concentrated, pre-sorted agro-industrial feedstocks
- Sugar mills are bulk generators under SWM Rules — legally mandated to process on-premises

**Opportunity framing for investors:**
- Maharashtra sugar mill CBG is a B2B tipping-fee model: the mill pays the venture to take press mud + spent wash, the venture produces CBG + sells to OMC
- This is a double-revenue model (tipping fee + CBG sale) — most economically attractive unit economics

**Why not beachhead**: Policy ecosystem weaker; state support less specific; MSW contamination in urban Maharashtra is high. Strong second-wave target after UP/Gujarat operations are proven.

### Recommended Beachhead: **Western UP — Muzaffarnagar/Meerut Triangle**

**Rationale:**
1. **Most active CBG ecosystem in India**: 32 plants operational, 113 under construction — means vendors, contractors, workers, and OMC relationships already exist in-state
2. **Multi-feedstock = resilience**: Cattle manure (year-round base) + paddy/wheat straw (seasonal supplement) + sugar press mud (Oct–Apr) = no single point of supply failure
3. **Government land + incentives**: State will offer land for cow dung CBG plants — lowers CAPEX threshold
4. **Proven benchmark adjacent**: HPCL Badaun (100 TPD, ₹133 crore) is 90 minutes from Muzaffarnagar — equipment suppliers, EPC contractors, grid connections already mapped
5. **Triple feedstock = triple B2B relationships**: Dairy farms, sugar mills, institutional food waste generators — three independent revenue-sharing agreements reduce concentration risk
6. **OMC offtake**: IOC/HPCL/BPCL have the most active CBG procurement in UP — shortest path to PPA/offtake agreement

**Phase 1 plant spec (recommended)**:
- Location: Muzaffarnagar or Meerut district
- Primary feedstock: Cattle manure (50 TPD), supplemented by press mud (20–30 TPD Oct–Apr), paddy straw (20–30 TPD Oct–Nov)
- Total input: ~80–100 TPD
- CBG output: ~10–14 TPD
- Co-product: ~50–65 TPD organic fertiliser (digestate)
- CAPEX: ₹80–120 crore (benchmarked against HPCL Badaun at ₹133 crore/100 TPD)
- Revenue: CBG @ ₹54/kg (SATAT floor) + digestate @ market; tipping fee from sugar mills if press mud included

**Second beachhead (Phase 2)**: Haryana Corridor — immediately adjacent geography, same technology, same OMC relationships, extends feedstock to pure straw and dairy to maximise economies of scale.

## 6. Market Structure & Competitive Landscape (Preview)

*Full analysis in WS7 — Competitor & Ecosystem Landscape. Key structural facts for cluster context:*

- **No dominant private aggregator**: The CBG market has no vertically integrated national champion. HPCL/IOC/BPCL are oil companies running pilots, not operators. Private players (EverEnviro, Pristine Organics, Attero) have 1–5 plants each.
- **Execution gap is the moat**: The 1,094 LoIs vs 108 commissioned ratio (10% execution rate) shows the problem is not capital or policy — it is **feedstock aggregation, EPC execution, and operations management**. A venture that solves this wins market share by default.
- **Cooperative model is defensible**: The Banas Dairy / AMUL model shows cooperatives are natural feedstock aggregators. Similar models can be replicated with dairy unions in UP/Haryana.
- **OMC relationships are not scarce**: SATAT guarantees any commissioned plant a purchase agreement. The bottleneck is plant commissioning, not offtake.
