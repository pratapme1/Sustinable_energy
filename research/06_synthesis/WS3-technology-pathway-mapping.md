## T1: Anaerobic Digestion (AD)

### What It Is
Biological decomposition of organic matter by microorganisms in the absence of oxygen, producing biogas (methane + CO₂) and digestate (biofertiliser). The most established wet organic waste-to-energy technology globally.

### Variants
| Variant | Description | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| Wet AD (CSTR) | Continuously stirred tank reactor; 8–12% TS feedstock | Food waste, manure, slurries |
| Dry AD | Higher solids (20–40% TS); plug-flow or batch | Crop residue, municipal waste |
| Co-digestion | Multiple feedstocks mixed for better yield | Manure + food waste + straw |
| UASB (Upflow Anaerobic Sludge Blanket) | For high-strength liquid waste | Distillery spent wash, dairy effluent |
| Two-stage AD | Hydrolysis + methanogenesis separated | Complex waste; improves yield |

### Energy Output
| Output | Description | Typical Specification |
|---|---|---|
| Raw biogas | 55–70% CH₄, 30–45% CO₂ | Used directly for cooking/heating |
| Compressed Biogas (CBG) | Purified, compressed to ~200 bar | Equivalent to CNG; vehicle fuel/pipeline |
| Biomethane / RNG | >97% CH₄; grid-injectable | Premium product; EU/US emerging |
| Electricity | Via gas engine/genset | 35–42% electrical efficiency |
| Heat (CHP) | Combined heat and power | 80–85% total efficiency (CHP) |

### Verified Energy Yields by Feedstock
| Feedstock | Biogas Yield | Methane Content | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Food waste (institutional) | 400–800 L/kg VS | 60–65% | Best yield; high VS content |
| Cattle/buffalo manure | 200–300 L/kg VS | 55–60% | Reliable; direct slurry input |
| Poultry manure | 300–500 L/kg VS | 60–65% | High NH₃ risk; dilution needed |
| Press mud (sugar mill) | 300–500 L/kg VS | 55–62% | Excellent; minimal preprocessing |
| Sewage sludge | 150–300 L/kg VS | 60–65% | Lower yield; large volume |
| Paddy straw (pretreated) | 250–350 L/kg VS | 52–58% | Needs pretreatment; co-digest with manure |
| Distillery spent wash | Very high (COD basis) | 55–60% | UASB reactor required |
| Fish/slaughterhouse waste | 400–600 L/kg VS | 60–68% | High protein = high yield |

### CAPEX (India-Verified) [Fact — High Confidence]
| Plant Size | CAPEX (INR) | CAPEX (USD) | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Small (1 TPD CBG output) | ₹3–5 crore | USD 360–600K | CSE / Renewable Watch 2024 |
| Medium (4.8 TPD CBG, SATAT standard) | ₹10–15 crore | USD 1.2–1.8M | MNRE CFA benchmark |
| Large (50–100 TPD waste input) | ₹30–80 crore | USD 3.6–9.6M | HPCL Badaun project: ₹133cr/100 TPD |
| Community/RWA (50–100 kg/day) | ₹5–15 lakh | USD 6,000–18,000 | BioCycle / Better India 2024 |

**Real project benchmark**: HPCL CBG plant, Badaun, UP (commissioned Jan 2024) — ₹133 crore investment, 100 TPD rice straw input, produces **14 TPD CBG + 65 TPD biomanure**

### OPEX (India)
- Small 1 TPD plant: ₹5–8 million/year (CSE)
- Monthly OPEX: ₹20–45 lakhs for medium-scale plants
- CAPEX = ~90% of total project cost; OPEX relatively lean
- Feedstock aggregation CAPEX: 30% of project cost for agri waste; lower for urban wet waste

### Government Support (India) [Fact — High Confidence]
| Scheme | Support |
|---|---|
| SATAT (MoPNG) | Purchase Agreement for CBG at OMC outlets; offtake guaranteed |
| MNRE CFA | Up to ₹4 crore per 4,800 kg/day capacity; max ₹10 crore/project |
| GOBARdhan | 500 new W2E plants announced Union Budget 2023 |
| IREDA financing | Loans up to 70% of project cost |
| Biomass Aggregation Machinery | 50% subsidy on BAM procurement (max ₹9 lakh/set) |
| SATAT CBG budget | ₹564.75 crore (2023-24 to 2026-27) |

### India Deployment Status [Fact — High Confidence]
- **103 operational CBG plants** as of 2024 (72 under SATAT)
- **1,094 active LoIs** issued under SATAT as of July 2025
- CBG sold FY 2023-24: **22,097 tonnes** (SATAT plants)
- CBG sold FY 2024-25: **42,800 tonnes** — doubling year-on-year
- Target: **5,000 plants by 2030** (revised from original 2025 target — severely behind)
- Leading states: UP (32 plants), Gujarat (20), Haryana (17)
- Gap: Target was 5,000 plants by 2023-24; only 108 commissioned — **execution gap = opportunity**

### Known Failure Modes
- Poor feedstock quality (high contamination, plastics in MSW)
- Inconsistent feedstock supply (seasonal agri waste)
- Ammonia inhibition (excess poultry/slaughter waste without dilution)
- Digestate disposal if no biofertiliser offtake arranged
- Biogas upgrading equipment failure (membrane, PSA)
- Under-sizing digesters for peak load

### Verdict for This Venture: ★★★★★ PRIMARY TECHNOLOGY
AD is the single most important technology for this venture. It is mature, policy-backed, and the correct match for the top 3 feedstocks (manure, institutional food waste, bulk generator wet waste). The SATAT execution gap is the market opportunity.

## T2: Gasification

### What It Is
Thermochemical conversion of carbonaceous material at high temperatures (700–1,200°C) with limited oxygen, producing **syngas** (CO + H₂ + CH₄). Syngas drives a gas engine for electricity or is cleaned and compressed for CNG/hydrogen.

### Variants
| Variant | Description | Scale |
|---|---|---|
| Downdraft gasifier | Fixed bed; simple; good gas quality | 10 kW–1 MW |
| Updraft gasifier | Higher tar content; simpler operation | 100 kW–10 MW |
| Fluidised bed | Uniform temperature; handles diverse fuels | 1–100 MW |
| Entrained flow | High temperature; clean syngas; complex | >50 MW |

### Best Feedstocks
- Rice husk (proven — thousands of units in India)
- Paddy and wheat straw (after densification)
- Cotton stalks, groundnut shells
- Municipal Solid Waste (as RDF — Refuse Derived Fuel)
- Wood and forest residue
- NOT suitable for: high-moisture feedstocks (food waste, manure) — requires <20–25% moisture

### Verified Energy Yields
- Syngas calorific value: **4–6 MJ/Nm³** (low-BTU gas)
- Electrical efficiency: **20–28%** (gas engine)
- Overall system efficiency: **70–80%** (including heat recovery)
- 1 tonne dry biomass → ~2–3 MWh electricity (rule of thumb)

### CAPEX (India)
| Scale | CAPEX (India) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Small (25–100 kW) | ₹25–150 lakh | Husk Power Systems range; rice husk |
| Medium (1–5 MW) | ₹1.5–8 crore | Per kWe: ₹2,500–15,000 (dual fuel to 100% gas) |
| Utility scale (10–50 MW) | ₹50–300 crore | Biomass power benchmark: ₹5–7 crore/MW |

### India Deployment [Fact — High Confidence]
- **Rice husk gasification**: most mature application; thousands of small units operational in Bihar, UP, Odisha
- Husk Power Systems (India startup): deployed 100+ village-scale gasifiers (25–100 kW)
- DESI Power, Ankur Scientific: active Indian manufacturers
- MNRE Biourja programme: supports syngas/producer gas projects
- Biomass power installed capacity (India): ~10,000 MW (most from combustion + bagasse cogen, not pure gasification)
- Challenge: Tar management in syngas; gas cleaning is critical for engine longevity

### Known Failure Modes
- Tar fouling of gas engines (major issue with updraft gasifiers)
- Feedstock moisture sensitivity (>25% moisture = poor performance)
- Ash clinkering at high temperatures (especially with high-potassium agri residue)
- Inconsistent feedstock sizing and quality
- Operator skill requirement is higher than AD

### Verdict for This Venture: ★★★☆☆ SECONDARY TECHNOLOGY
Important for dry agri residue (paddy straw, cotton stalks) that AD cannot handle efficiently. Best deployed at aggregation hubs — rice mills, cotton ginning clusters, mandis. Not the primary entry point but critical for scaling into agri residue in Phase 2.

## T3: Pyrolysis

### What It Is
Thermal decomposition of organic material in the **complete absence of oxygen** at 300–700°C, producing three co-products: **bio-oil** (liquid), **biochar** (solid carbon), and **syngas** (non-condensable gas). Product distribution depends on temperature and residence time.

### Variants
| Type | Temperature | Products | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Slow pyrolysis | 300–500°C | High biochar (35–40%) | Carbon sequestration focus |
| Fast pyrolysis | 400–600°C | High bio-oil (60–75%) | Liquid fuel focus |
| Flash pyrolysis | >600°C | Maximum bio-oil | Very fast reaction; complex |
| Torrefaction | 200–300°C | Torrefied biomass | Intermediate product; better fuel |
| Hydrothermal (HTC) | 180–250°C + pressure | Hydrochar | Works with wet feedstock |

### Energy Outputs
- **Bio-oil**: 15–20 MJ/kg calorific value; can substitute heavy fuel oil; needs upgrading for transport fuel
- **Biochar**: 25–35 MJ/kg; carbon sequestration value + soil amendment premium
- **Syngas**: 4–6 MJ/Nm³; used internally for process heat
- Typical split (fast pyrolysis): 60–75% bio-oil, 15–25% biochar, 10–20% syngas

### CAPEX
| Scale | CAPEX (Global) | India Estimate | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Small (1–5 TPD) | USD 50,000–200,000 | ₹25–80 lakh | Entrepreneur India 2026 |
| Medium (10 TPD) | USD 100,000–400,000 | ₹1–3 crore | MDPI 2024 review |
| Large (50+ TPD) | USD 5–20M | ₹40–160 crore | Limited commercial examples |

### Biochar Value in India
- Production cost: ₹1,000–3,500/tonne biomass input
- Biochar market price: USD 188–260/tonne (global minimum selling price)
- India biochar market: nascent but growing with soil health awareness
- **Premium opportunity**: Carbon credit value of biochar = USD 50–200/tonne CO₂ sequestered

### India Deployment Status
- Small-scale biochar units exist (cottage-level kiln technology is ancient)
- **No large commercial pyrolysis plants** for energy recovery in India as of 2024
- A few pilot projects at IITs and MNRE-supported labs
- TRL in India context: **5–6** (lab/pilot; not commercial scale)

### Known Failure Modes
- Bio-oil is acidic, viscous, unstable — requires costly upgrading for transport fuel use
- Biochar market is price-sensitive and quality-dependent
- Not suitable for wet feedstock without pre-drying
- High CAPEX relative to energy output at small scale

### Verdict for This Venture: ★★☆☆☆ NICHE / PHASE 3
Interesting for biochar production (soil amendment + carbon credits) from agri residue. Not a primary energy pathway for first-wedge. Consider as a value-add product line from agri residue after gasification infrastructure is established.

## T4: Direct Combustion / Incineration (WTE)

### What It Is
Controlled burning of waste at 850–1,200°C to generate steam, which drives a turbine for electricity generation. The dominant WTE technology globally (>1,700 plants worldwide).

### Energy Output
- Electrical efficiency: **20–30%** (net)
- Combined heat and power (CHP): up to **80–85%** total efficiency
- Typical: 1 tonne MSW → **400–600 kWh electricity**
- Net calorific requirement: >7 MJ/kg waste (India's MSW at 1,751 kcal/kg = ~7.3 MJ/kg — borderline)

### CAPEX
| Scale | CAPEX (India/Global) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Small municipal (10–50 TPD) | Not viable (too small for thermal) | Minimum economic scale ~200 TPD |
| Medium (200–500 TPD) | ₹150–400 crore | Indian benchmark |
| Large (1,000+ TPD) | ₹800 crore – ₹2,000+ crore | Mumbai, Delhi WTE projects |

### India Deployment [Fact — High Confidence]
- **10+ large WTE combustion plants** in India (Delhi, Mumbai, Pune, Chennai)
- Delhi: East Delhi WTE Plant (1,300 TPD); West Delhi WTE Plant (1,950 TPD)
- Persistent challenges: **low calorific value of Indian MSW**, high moisture, poor segregation
- Multiple plants running below design capacity or shut down periodically
- Criticism: locked-in contract model creates disincentive for waste reduction
- Global: >1,700 plants; >80% in developed countries (Japan, France, Germany, US)

### Critical Problem for India Context
Indian MSW averages **47% moisture and 1,751 kcal/kg** calorific value. Most combustion plants require **>8.5 MJ/kg (2,030 kcal/kg)** for self-sustaining combustion. India's wet, high-organic waste is **not ideal for incineration** — it requires auxiliary fuel or intensive RDF processing.

### Known Failure Modes
- High moisture Indian waste requires auxiliary fuel — increases cost dramatically
- Dioxin/furan emissions if combustion temperature not maintained (850°C+)
- Fly ash disposal is hazardous and costly
- Large minimum economic scale (>200 TPD) — not suitable for decentralised model
- Political resistance due to air quality concerns near communities

### Verdict for This Venture: ★★☆☆☆ NOT RECOMMENDED FOR FIRST ENTRY
Large scale, high CAPEX, requires government long-term contract, operational challenges with Indian MSW quality. Large players (IL&FS, A2Z, Essel Group) already in this space. Not a viable first-entry for a founder-led venture.

## T5: Briquetting and Pelletisation

### What It Is
Mechanical densification of dry biomass residue (agri straw, husk, stalks) into standardised **solid fuel blocks (briquettes)** or **cylindrical pellets** for use in industrial boilers, co-firing with coal, or direct combustion.

### Energy Output
- Briquette calorific value: **3,200–4,200 kcal/kg** (vs coal: 5,000–7,000 kcal/kg)
- Pellet calorific value: **3,800–4,500 kcal/kg**
- Used as: coal substitute in boilers, kilns, brick firing, institutional cooking

### CAPEX (India) [Fact — High Confidence]
| Scale | CAPEX | MNRE Subsidy |
|---|---|---|
| Small (0.5 TPH machine) | ₹9–15 lakh | ₹9–21 lakh per TPH capacity |
| Medium plant (2–5 TPH) | ₹30–80 lakh | Max ₹1.05 crore per plant |
| Full production plant (5 TPH) | ₹1–2 crore | 30% of plant & machinery cost |

### Market Prices (India 2024) [Fact — High Confidence]
- Briquettes: **₹6,000–8,000/tonne**
- Pellets: **₹10,000–14,000/tonne**
- Production cost: **₹4,000–6,000/tonne** (including biomass at ₹1,000–3,500/tonne)
- India biomass pellets market: **USD 552 million (2023)** → projected USD 1.2 billion by 2035
- NTPC co-firing mandate: 5–7% biomass co-firing in coal plants → **massive guaranteed demand**

### India Deployment [Fact — High Confidence]
- Active, mature industry — thousands of briquetting units across India
- NTPC co-firing policy (2021): mandates biomass pellet use in coal plants — creates guaranteed offtake
- Key feedstocks: paddy straw, rice husk, cotton stalks, sugarcane bagasse, groundnut shells
- Geographic concentration: Punjab, Haryana, UP (paddy/wheat straw), Gujarat (cotton), AP (groundnut)

### Known Failure Modes
- Feedstock quality variation → inconsistent product quality
- Moisture sensitivity — biomass must be dry (<15%)
- Competition with farm use (cattle fodder) for straw
- Price competition with cheap coal in some regions

### Verdict for This Venture: ★★★★☆ STRONG SECONDARY PATHWAY
Briquetting/pelletisation is the **lowest-complexity, fastest-to-deploy** agri residue technology. CAPEX is low, technology is proven, and NTPC co-firing mandate creates guaranteed demand. Best as a feeder business for farmers while building toward more complex AD/gasification infrastructure.

## T6: Bioethanol / Fermentation

### What It Is
Biological fermentation of sugars (from sugarcane, corn, starchy crops) or cellulosic biomass (lignocellulosic second-generation) to produce **ethanol** as liquid transport fuel.

### Variants
| Generation | Feedstock | Maturity | India Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1G (First gen) | Sugarcane juice, molasses, grain | TRL 9 — fully commercial | Very active — 700+ distilleries |
| 2G (Second gen) | Paddy straw, wheat straw, bagasse cellulose | TRL 6–7 — early commercial | 2–3 pilot plants; HPCL, IOCL projects |
| 3G | Algae | TRL 3–4 | Lab stage only |

### India Context [Fact — High Confidence]
- India ethanol blending target: **20% by 2025** (E20); currently ~15–16% (2024)
- 1G ethanol production: well-established; sugarcane molasses dominant
- 2G ethanol: **HPCL's 2G ethanol plant, Bathinda** (100 KLD capacity, paddy straw feedstock) — India's first commercial 2G plant
- IOCL Panipat: 100 KLD 2G biorefinery from paddy straw (commissioned 2022)
- Production cost 2G ethanol: **₹65–90/litre** vs petrol ~₹100/litre — competitive with subsidy

### Key Challenge
Lignocellulosic bioethanol (2G) requires: pretreatment + enzymatic hydrolysis + fermentation = 3-step process with high CAPEX and enzyme cost. Not yet cost-competitive without blending mandates and carbon credits.

### Verdict for This Venture: ★★★☆☆ MONITOR, NOT PRIMARY
1G ethanol is dominated by large sugar companies. 2G ethanol is capital-intensive and technically complex. Not a first-wedge opportunity. Relevant if the venture develops into a full biorefinery platform in Phase 3+.

## T7: Landfill Gas (LFG) Capture

### What It Is
Passive capture of methane naturally generated from decomposing organic waste in existing landfills. Captured gas upgraded to biogas/electricity.

### Energy Potential (India) [Fact — Medium Confidence]
- India landfill methane: **990–1,084 Gg/year** (MDPI 2023)
- Theoretical energy equivalent: very large
- LFG capture potential: **IEA estimates India has significant uncaptured LFG**

### India Deployment Reality [Fact — High Confidence]
- **Not a single landfill in India** has an LFG-to-energy facility (CPCB study — 7,000+ sites)
- GAIL pilot project at Ghazipur Delhi dump: pilot/demonstration only
- No national action plan for LFG recovery
- No specific legislation mandating LFG capture
- Reason for inaction: open dumps (not engineered landfills) cannot support gas capture infrastructure

### Verdict for This Venture: ★★☆☆☆ FUTURE OPPORTUNITY, NOT NOW
Massive theoretical potential but zero infrastructure for capture. Would require government mandate, engineered landfill retrofitting, and long policy timeline. Not actionable for a startup in next 5 years. Flag for carbon credit strategy later.

## T8: Hydrothermal Liquefaction (HTL)

### What It Is
Thermochemical conversion of **wet organic material** (sewage sludge, wet biomass, food waste) at **250–374°C and 100–300 bar pressure** into **bio-crude oil**, water phase, gas, and solid residue. Eliminates need for pre-drying.

### Energy Output
- Bio-crude yield: **19–60% mass yield** depending on feedstock
- Bio-crude calorific value: ~35 MJ/kg (comparable to crude oil)
- Minimum fuel selling price: **USD 0.61–1.29/litre gasoline equivalent** (commercial scale)

### TRL and Deployment
- **Global TRL: 4–5** — pilot scale; not yet commercial
- Key players: PNNL (US), Aarhus University (Denmark), Licella (Australia)
- No commercial-scale HTL plants operating anywhere in 2024
- India: **not deployed; no known projects**

### Why It Matters Long-Term
- Only technology that can handle **high-moisture waste** (sewage sludge, wet food waste) thermochemically without pre-drying
- Potentially competitive with fossil crude at commercial scale
- Strong candidate for municipal sewage sludge to liquid fuel (large STP clusters)

### Verdict for This Venture: ★★☆☆☆ WATCH LIST — NOT YET
Technology not commercial anywhere. Not suitable for a market-entry pitch. Include as a "future technology" note in the final document. Revisit in 5–7 years.

## T9: Biomass-to-Hydrogen (Bio-H₂)

### What It Is
Production of hydrogen gas from organic waste via:
- **Dark fermentation**: microbial H₂ production from organic waste (low yield)
- **Gasification + water gas shift**: syngas → H₂ (most mature route)
- **Steam reforming of biogas**: biogas → H₂ + CO₂

### India Context [Fact — High Confidence]
- National Green Hydrogen Mission: **₹19,744 crore** allocated (Jan 2023)
- Biomass-to-H₂ funding: up to **₹25 crore/project**, 80% equipment cost support for private sector
- Current green H₂ cost: **₹397–560/kg** (USD 4.6–6.7/kg) — 2–3x grey hydrogen cost
- Dark fermentation H₂ yield: **0.004–0.044 kg H₂/kg biomass** — extremely low
- Gasification route is most mature bio-H₂ pathway

### Verdict for This Venture: ★★☆☆☆ LONG-TERM ADJACENCY
Relevant as India's hydrogen economy develops. Gasification infrastructure built for agri residue could be retrofit for H₂ production in Phase 3+. Not a first-entry play — economics not competitive without significant policy support beyond current levels.

## Key Technology Findings for Venture Strategy

### Finding 1: AD is the Unambiguous Primary Technology
For wet organic waste and manure — which are the top 3 first-wedge feedstocks — AD/CBG is the only technology that is simultaneously:
- Commercially mature (TRL 9)
- Policy-backed (SATAT, MNRE CFA, GOBARdhan)
- Financially viable at small-medium scale
- Proven in India (103 plants operational)
- Producing a premium product (CBG at OMC-guaranteed offtake price)

### Finding 2: The SATAT Gap Is the Opportunity
India targets 5,000 CBG plants. Only 108 commissioned as of 2025. The technology works — the execution is missing. A venture with strong operations capability, feedstock aggregation model, and project development discipline can capture this white space at scale.

### Finding 3: Briquetting Is the Fastest Path into Agri Residue
For paddy straw, rice husk, cotton stalks — briquetting/pelletisation is the lowest-CAPEX, lowest-risk, fastest-to-revenue path. NTPC co-firing mandate creates guaranteed demand. Use this as a feeder to establish farmer relationships before investing in gasification.

### Finding 4: Two-Technology Platform Is Ideal
- **Technology 1**: AD → CBG (wet waste, manure, food waste)
- **Technology 2**: Briquetting/Pelletisation → solid fuel (dry agri residue)
These two technologies together cover **80%+ of India's organic waste** with proven commercial pathways, without requiring any emerging or unproven technology.

### Finding 5: Avoid Direct Combustion/WTE for Founder Entry
Large scale, dominated by established players, operational challenges with Indian MSW quality, and no differentiation pathway for a new venture. Leave to IL&FS, A2Z, and municipal tender operators.
