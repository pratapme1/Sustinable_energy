# India Waste-to-Energy Research Blueprint

## Purpose

This blueprint adapts the generic research framework in `docs/end-to-end-research-framework-blueprint.md` to a specific founder-level project:

- market focus starts with India
- technology benchmarking can be global
- the target outcome is energy generation from waste
- the final output must be strong enough for investor conversations and also understandable to government stakeholders and citizens

This is not a general sustainability document.
It is a decision and pitch support document for building a waste-to-energy opportunity thesis.

## Project Framing

### Working Thesis

Humans will always need energy, water, food, and clean air.
Humans also generate unavoidable waste.
Within that waste stream, organic wet waste and agricultural waste create both a disposal problem and a potential energy opportunity.
If the right waste streams, geographies, technologies, and business models are selected, waste can be converted into usable energy products with economic, environmental, and social value.

For this project, "fuel" should be interpreted broadly as energy output.
That includes gaseous fuels, electricity, heat, solid bioenergy formats, and other energy carriers where technically and commercially relevant.

### Core Research Objective

Produce a detailed, evidence-backed project document that answers whether an India-first waste-to-energy venture should be built, where it should start, what waste streams it should target, which energy pathways are most viable, and what business model and implementation approach are investable.

### Decision To Support

The research must support these decisions:

1. Which waste streams should be prioritized first?
2. Which energy products are the best target outputs?
3. Which technologies are best matched to those waste streams?
4. Which geographies in India are the best beachhead markets?
5. What business model is most attractive?
6. What would make the opportunity investable versus not investable?

### Primary Audiences

- founder: strategic direction, entry point, venture scope
- investors: market size, defensibility, economics, risks, scalability
- government: public benefit, waste reduction, policy fit, infrastructure alignment
- citizens: social value, health impact, cleaner cities and villages, local benefit

## Key Change To The Generic Workflow

For this project, the generic workflow needs two extra front-end filters before broad market analysis:

1. Waste stream segmentation
   Do not treat "waste" as one market. Wet municipal waste, manure, crop residue, food processing waste, sewage sludge, bagasse, paddy straw, and press mud behave differently.

2. Energy pathway matching
   Do not study technology in isolation. The research must map waste type to feasible energy output.

Without these two steps, the project becomes too broad and the final pitch becomes weak.

## Scope

### In Scope

- India market as the initial commercialization focus
- global technology scan and best-practice benchmarking
- wet waste and agricultural waste as the main feedstock families
- energy outputs such as biogas, compressed biogas, renewable natural gas, electricity, heat, syngas, pellets, bio-oil, ethanol, and other relevant energy carriers where technically appropriate
- market, technology, economics, competition, implementation, policy, and risk
- founder and investor decision support

### Out Of Scope

- all waste categories outside the project thesis unless they directly affect competitiveness
- detailed engineering design for a single plant
- full EPC specification
- premature expansion to too many countries before India is understood

## Final Deliverable

The final research-based project document should read like an investor-grade market and venture thesis.

It should answer:

- what problem exists
- how large the opportunity is
- which waste streams matter
- what energy can be generated from them
- where that opportunity is concentrated
- which players already exist
- what technologies are available globally
- what economics, operating model, and partnerships are needed
- what risks could break the thesis
- what the recommended entry strategy is

## Required Outputs

At minimum, this project should produce:

- research brief
- India waste stream taxonomy
- waste-to-energy pathway map
- state and cluster prioritization map
- competitor and ecosystem map
- market size model
- unit economics model
- implementation and operating model
- policy and incentive matrix
- risk register
- final investor-grade project document

## Core Research Questions

### Workstream 1: Problem Space And Why Now

### Objective

Show that waste is a real, large, urgent problem and that the timing for solving it through energy conversion is credible.

### Questions

- How large is India’s waste challenge today?
- What share of that challenge comes from wet waste and agricultural residues?
- What happens to these waste streams today?
- What environmental, health, and economic damage is caused by current disposal methods?
- Why is now the right time for waste-to-energy solutions?

### Expected Outputs

- problem statement
- urgency narrative
- quantified size of the waste burden
- "why now" section for the pitch

### Workstream 2: Waste Stream Segmentation

### Objective

Create a defensible classification of target feedstocks.

### Feedstock Families To Study

- municipal wet waste: household food waste, market waste, hotel and restaurant waste
- institutional wet waste: campuses, hospitals, canteens, malls
- agricultural wet waste: manure, slurry, organic processing waste
- agricultural residue: paddy straw, husk, bagasse, stubble, cane trash, shells, stalks
- food processing and agro-industrial byproducts: distillery waste, press mud, dairy waste, slaughter waste, fruit and vegetable processing waste
- sewage sludge and related organics where relevant to the business case

### Questions

- What are the major feedstock classes?
- What are their typical physical and chemical characteristics?
- Which are wet, dry, seasonal, contaminated, concentrated, or dispersed?
- Which are easy versus hard to collect?
- Which create the strongest disposal pain?

### Expected Outputs

- taxonomy table
- feedstock property matrix
- collection and logistics notes
- shortlist of the most attractive feedstocks

### Workstream 3: Waste-To-Energy Pathway Matching

### Objective

Map each waste class to the energy outputs and technologies it can realistically support.

### Pathways To Evaluate

- anaerobic digestion and biomethanation
- compressed biogas and renewable natural gas upgrading
- compost plus energy-integrated models where relevant
- gasification
- pyrolysis
- briquetting and pelletization
- direct combustion or co-firing
- ethanol and advanced biofuel pathways
- hybrid or cluster-based models

### Questions

- Which technologies best fit which feedstocks?
- What energy products can each pathway generate?
- What pre-processing is required?
- What maturity level does each pathway have globally and in India?
- What technical failure modes are common?

### Expected Outputs

- waste-to-energy map
- technology maturity table
- pathway comparison matrix
- shortlist of technology families worth deep diligence

### Workstream 4: India Market Baseline

### Objective

Quantify the India opportunity and identify where it is concentrated.

### Questions

- How much target waste exists in India by category?
- Which states, cities, districts, crop belts, or industrial clusters matter most?
- How fragmented is supply?
- How much of the waste is already collected, traded, or processed?
- Where are the largest unserved or underserved pockets?

### Geographic Lens

The research should move from national to cluster level:

1. India overall
2. state-level opportunity
3. district, city, or industrial-cluster prioritization
4. candidate beachhead clusters

### Expected Outputs

- India market baseline
- state ranking
- cluster heat map
- first-wave geographic shortlist

### Workstream 5: Global Technology Benchmark

### Objective

Bring in the best relevant technology, operating, and commercial lessons from anywhere, but only where they can translate into India.

### Questions

- Which countries or companies have solved similar feedstock-to-energy problems well?
- Which technology configurations actually operate at scale?
- What operating metrics matter most?
- What lessons transfer to India and what does not transfer?
- Which vendors, licensors, or partners should be watched?

### Expected Outputs

- global benchmark set
- transferability assessment for India
- vendor and partner longlist

### Workstream 6: Customer, Offtake, And Revenue Model

### Objective

Define who pays, why they pay, and how revenue is created.

### Revenue Pools To Evaluate

- tipping or waste processing fees
- sale of biogas, CBG, RNG, or pipeline gas
- electricity sales
- heat or steam sales
- sale of pellets, briquettes, or other energy products
- co-products such as digestate, compost, or carbon credits where relevant
- government incentives or viability support where material

### Questions

- Who are the waste suppliers?
- Who are the energy buyers or offtakers?
- Can the model earn from both waste intake and energy output?
- What price realization is realistic?
- Which revenue streams are stable and which are volatile?

### Expected Outputs

- customer map
- revenue stack
- offtake structure options
- business model options

### Workstream 7: Competitor And Ecosystem Landscape

### Objective

Understand the current playing field and identify whitespace.

### Questions

- Who are the current players in India?
- What business models are they using?
- Which waste streams and geographies are already crowded?
- Where are there execution gaps?
- Which ecosystem actors matter: municipalities, FPOs, sugar mills, mandis, waste aggregators, gas distributors, EPC firms, technology vendors, carbon market players?

### Expected Outputs

- player map
- competitor profiles
- ecosystem map
- whitespace assessment

### Workstream 8: Economics And Investability

### Objective

Convert the opportunity into an investor-usable business case.

### Questions

- What are the main cost buckets: collection, transport, preprocessing, plant CAPEX, OPEX, manpower, maintenance, quality control, compliance?
- How do economics vary by feedstock and plant scale?
- What utilization level is needed?
- What are the break-even points and sensitivities?
- Which assumptions matter most?
- What returns could an investor plausibly expect?

### Expected Outputs

- unit economics model
- scenario model
- CAPEX and OPEX framework
- sensitivity analysis
- investability summary

### Workstream 9: Implementation And Operating Model

### Objective

Explain how this gets built and run in the real world.

### Questions

- What does the end-to-end value chain look like?
- Who owns collection, transport, preprocessing, conversion, offtake, and maintenance?
- What infrastructure and permits are needed?
- What talent and capabilities are required?
- What deployment model is best: centralized plant, decentralized units, hub-and-spoke, partnership-led, or embedded industrial model?

### Expected Outputs

- operating model
- implementation roadmap
- capability map
- deployment model comparison

### Workstream 10: Policy, Regulation, And Public Value

### Objective

Show how the venture fits India’s policy environment and public-interest narrative.

### Questions

- What national and state policies support or constrain the model?
- Which subsidies, mandates, procurement programs, or blending targets matter?
- What waste handling and environmental compliance rules apply?
- How should the project be positioned for government stakeholders?
- What citizen-facing benefits are strongest: cleaner surroundings, reduced burning, lower emissions, better public health, rural income, energy access?

### Expected Outputs

- policy and incentive matrix
- compliance checklist
- public value narrative

### Workstream 11: Risks, Failure Modes, And Diligence

### Objective

Make the pitch credible by showing what could fail.

### Risk Categories

- feedstock supply risk
- contamination risk
- technology underperformance
- collection failure
- price volatility
- policy change
- execution and project delay
- financing risk
- offtake risk
- community acceptance risk

### Expected Outputs

- risk register
- mitigation plan
- "what would break the thesis" section

### Workstream 12: Entry Strategy Recommendation

### Objective

Convert the research into a clear go or no-go and a focused entry plan.

### Questions

- What is the best first beachhead?
- Which feedstock-pathway-geography combination should be prioritized?
- Should the company be a developer, operator, technology integrator, marketplace, aggregator, or hybrid?
- What partnerships are required before launch?
- What proof points are required before raising capital?

### Expected Outputs

- recommended entry wedge
- phased roadmap
- near-term milestones
- capital raise narrative

## Research Sequence

The work should be done in this order:

### Phase A: Frame The Venture Question

- finalize the research brief
- define the decision to support
- list the core hypotheses

### Phase B: Segment Waste And Match Pathways

- classify feedstocks
- map feedstocks to energy pathways
- eliminate weak or nonviable combinations early

### Phase C: Quantify India And Prioritize Clusters

- size the waste and energy opportunity
- rank states and clusters
- shortlist entry geographies

### Phase D: Benchmark Technology And Players Globally

- study proven models globally
- identify what can transfer to India
- assess current Indian competition and gaps

### Phase E: Build Economics And Operating Model

- construct unit economics
- compare deployment models
- define revenue stack and partnerships

### Phase F: Build The Pitch Thesis

- synthesize the evidence
- challenge the weak assumptions
- prepare the final project document

- feedstock consistency
- moisture and contamination suitability
- collection complexity
- technology maturity

## Structure Of The Final Investor-Grade Document

The final pitch or project dossier should be structured like this:

1. Executive summary
2. Vision and thesis
3. Problem space and urgency
4. India waste landscape
5. Target waste streams
6. Waste-to-energy pathway map
7. Market size and cluster prioritization
8. Global benchmark and technology options
9. India player and ecosystem landscape
10. Business model and revenue stack
11. Unit economics and financial logic
12. Implementation and operating model
13. Policy, regulation, and public value
14. Risks and mitigation
15. Recommended entry strategy
16. Roadmap and capital needs
17. Appendix, methods, and sources

## Evidence Standard For This Project

High-value claims in the final document should be backed by:

- official Indian government data where available
- reputable international and industry datasets
- company filings and project disclosures
- credible technology benchmarks
- primary interviews where possible

Claims about market size, cost, yields, and policy support should never rely only on blogs or AI summaries.

## First Three Research Loops

Start with these loops before expanding:

### Loop 1

- quantify India wet waste and agricultural waste categories
- map current disposal methods
- identify the most painful waste clusters

### Loop 2

- map each major waste class to viable energy outputs
- identify the top 3 to 5 feedstock-pathway combinations

### Loop 3

- size the market and economics for those shortlisted combinations
- identify the strongest first beachhead in India

## Definition Of Done

This research is done when the project can answer, with evidence:

- which waste streams to target first
- which energy product to produce first
- which technology route is best suited
- where in India to start
- why the opportunity is large enough
- why the economics can work
- why the model is differentiated
- what must be proven next before capital is raised

The document should leave an investor, founder, government stakeholder, or citizen with a clear understanding of the opportunity, the constraints, and the logic for the recommended path.
